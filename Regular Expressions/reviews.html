5.0 out of 5 starsGreat running watch, eliminates the HRM chest strap.
ByM. Helmickon June 15, 2015
First off - yes, I do have one of these even they they aren't for sale online yet. Garmin had a booth at the expo for the Seattle Rock 'n' Roll marathon and I as able to pick one up. So yes, I do have one, and I have run with it.

It is a great running watch. The GPS seems fairly accurate, over reporting a bit, but less so than what I was getting when using an app on my phone. The watch report 26.77 total miles for the marathon. The HR function seem very accurate comparable to my previous polar chest strap setup. I like having a log and graph to look at afterwards for the HR metrics, something I never had before.

Wearing it for the first time, the watch felt a little heavy, but after wearing it for a full day I no longer noticed it.
The post-run analysis with the Garmin tools is great.

I did misconfigure the watch for my marathon, and that got annoying. I had experimented with pace alarms. This caused the watch to beep at me a lot over the last 6 miles of the race when pace dropped. Oh well - I won't be using that unless I am specifically pace training.

The only complains I have are around the Garmin Connect mobile app. There are a few things that are broken
- Live tracking to Facebook is currently broken, they have acknowledged this in their online forums.
- Yet another exercise app that tries to build their own social network - this is a mistake.
- No calendar view in the mobile app - this requires use a computer/Web browser to set up your workout calendar and send it down to the watch

In the end - I'm really happy to be free of the HRM chest strap and to no longer be tied to running with my phone for GPS tracking.

2.0 out of 5 starsSo-So...will be returned.
ByT. Carlsonon July 5, 2015
Long time user of Garmin products. Prior watches were the ForeRunner 610 and the ForeRunner 220.

The best I can give this watch w/o the HRM strap is an 'OK'. The optical sensor doesn't do well keeping accurate HR data unless you tighten the wrist strap to an uncomfortable position because any light under the base will cause error (it's not a small wrist issue, either - I'm 6'4" 200#). This is especially problematic for non-running exercises (weight training, racquetball, etc.). Garmin has seemingly built in logic to cloak HR data errors - there aren't huge swings in HR data like you'd occasionally see with an HRM strap. Feels a little disingenuous to me.

I've worn both the 225 and the 220 at the same time. Results are sometimes similar, mostly when running with a tight 225 band. Anything else, there is a 10-15% delta in HR and related data. I trust the 220 data much more.

You can pair a HR strap to the 225 to avoid the optical sensor issues. But if you're going to do that, save your money and go with the reliable 220.

UPDATE: This is going back. The optical HR just doesn't work (74 BPM on hill repeats, 188 BPM on stroll walk). I've been fighting with it for months with hope that it would stabilize. I'm really disappointed. Will try again on the next iteration - this one is a beta and it operates like it.

4.0 out of 5 starsVery Good
ByJohn McCarthyon February 2, 2016
Color: Black/RedStyle Name: U.S. VersionVerified Purchase
Works great. Quick to connect to GPS. It changes the time based on the local GPS. It's more geared towards running, but it captures any activity that involves moving around. Charges quickly, lasts a long time. Syncs just fine over my computer or iOS device. Heart rate seems accurate enough considering there is no strap. I don't like it as an activity monitor as much as I liked the FitBit Surge, but the Garmin works well enough. I use this more than the FitBit Surge, and I think it's because the Garmin 225 is more like a watch where the FitBit looks more like an activity monitor. The Surge is better for other non-running activities since the Garmin has such a running focus. That just means the Garmin kind-of assumes everything is a run, but the Surge asks you how to "label" the activity you're about to start. I do a lot of different activities; with the Garmin, everything is labeled as a run even though I'm biking or walking. But I'm admitting that I still tend to wear the Garmin more, even with this little caveat.

ByO. Reynoldson January 29, 2016
Color: Black/RedStyle Name: U.S. Version
The heart rate monitor cannot be counted on to give accurate readings. Sometimes it does, but other times it is laughably off (watch said 120 bpm while rate measured by jugular timing was more like 160). Then other times the two readings agree. I have tried wearing the watch snugly and very snugly.. still erratic accuracy. And a friend of mine who tried it had the same results.
-The software is not easy to learn to use, and the manual that came with the watch is not much help. However, through much trial and error I finally figured it out.
The GPS seems to work well, as does the altitutde, altough I have not really tested these. People seem to like the way it looks.
The heart rate monitor is an important feature for me, so I plan to return it.
